Built With AI, Here to Stay

VIJAY PANANGIPALLY · JULY 10, 2026 · 7 MIN READ

The moment someone learns a piece of software was built with AI assistance, something shifts in how they look at it. Not a change in what they see: the interface hasn't moved, the feature still works exactly as it did a second ago. What changes is the presumption underneath it. Suddenly it's "vibe coded," a toy, a liability waiting to be discovered, something to be politely impressed by rather than actually trusted. The verdict arrives before the inspection: before anyone has opened the app, clicked through a flow, or asked what happens when something breaks. It is a strange kind of skepticism, because most of the people extending it are, in the very same week, leaning on AI themselves to draft an email, summarize a document, or debug their own code, and extending themselves plenty of grace for it. The assumption seems to be that when AI is involved, the judgment, the trade-offs, the decisions under pressure, all the things that actually make software trustworthy, must have gone missing too. That assumption is wrong often enough that it's worth examining closely. And it matters because AI-assisted development isn't a passing trend to wait out. It's already how a great deal of serious software gets built, ours included, and the habit of judging the method instead of the result needs to catch up to that fact.

The tool started with a spreadsheet. The IRCC PR Master Tracker is a Google Sheet that hundreds of Canadian Express Entry applicants collaboratively maintain, each row someone else's journey: when they got their Acknowledgement of Receipt, when biometrics were requested, when their background check cleared, when the final decision landed. It is remarkable because it is real, the collective lived experience of people who went through this before you, not a government estimate. But the one question that mattered most was also the hardest to answer from inside it: how much was actually moving. How many people had an update this week. How many cases had progressed to the next stage since you last looked. In a spreadsheet with hundreds of rows and no real filtering, where sorting by one column broke the grouping of another, that pulse was invisible. You could not glance at it and know whether the process was moving at all; you could only scroll, guess, and scroll again.

We built Track Your App for the people staring at that spreadsheet at midnight, doing quiet arithmetic on someone else's timeline to guess at their own. Waiting on a permanent residency decision is not a background process. It sits with you at dinner, it wakes you up, and the only honest comfort available is knowing how long it actually took for people whose situation looked like yours. That is what the spreadsheet held, buried under its own size. We wanted to hand it back to people in a form that actually answered the question they came with.

"AI-assisted" did not mean typing a sentence and receiving an app. It meant sitting with an AI collaborator through the same decisions any serious build requires, and living with the consequences of getting them wrong. Early on we had to choose where the data would live, and the obvious modern choice, a popular database service, paused itself automatically after about a week without traffic on its free tier. For most side projects that is a footnote. For something people check anxiously, sometimes disappear from for weeks and return to at the worst possible moment, a database that might be asleep when they need it is not acceptable. We chose a less fashionable option that never sleeps, because the people using this could not be expected to work around our infrastructure. That choice held until the free tier itself became the problem: once enough real timelines were flowing through it, the strict computing limit on that tier made the site start timing out under ordinary traffic, an outage that was entirely ours to own and fix. We moved to a paid tier the same week and the ceiling disappeared.

The hardest call we made was not architectural at all, it was ethical. People trusting a stranger's app with details of their immigration status deserved better than having their name, their email, or any real identifying detail sitting in a database that could someday be breached, subpoenaed, or simply misused. So partway through the build we tore out how accounts worked and rebuilt them from nothing: no email stored anywhere, no real name, just a nickname each person could choose for themselves or let us generate. It cost us weeks we had not planned to spend, on a feature nobody using the app would ever notice as a feature. We did it anyway because it was the right call, not because anyone asked for it.

That rebuild meant restructuring the one table every other piece of the system depended on. Partway through, a safety guard that existed specifically to stop a rebuild from cascading into connected tables got removed, on the assumption it was no longer needed. It was needed. When the rebuild ran, it cascaded further than intended and deleted real users' application records: actual people's actual immigration milestones, the dates they had trusted us to hold onto, gone in the time it takes a query to execute.

We caught it within minutes, and nothing was permanently lost. Every table had automatic point-in-time backups running underneath it, so we restored from the moment before the mistake, carefully rebuilt everything that had changed since, and verified every record was back exactly as it should be, milestone by milestone, application by application. The lesson we carry forward is a plain one: before you touch anything a table depends on, back up everything connected to it, not only the piece you meant to change. It is the kind of lesson most engineering teams learn once, the hard way, and we were fortunate it cost us an afternoon of quiet panic instead of a single person's trust.

None of that happened over a weekend. Months later, the web app has been live and growing the whole time, over six hundred real applicants have shared their own timelines through it, thousands of automated tests run against every change before it ships, and a native iOS app is now sitting with Apple, waiting on review, built the same way: decision by decision, incident by incident.

Nobody who dismisses a piece of AI-assisted software at first mention has any of that in view. Not the reasoning behind choosing a database, not the ethical call to strip out personal data nobody demanded we strip, not the night we nearly lost real people's records and the hours spent getting every one of them back, not the tests, not the months. What is visible, the only thing visible, is the phrase itself: built with AI. And that phrase alone is apparently enough to render a verdict, long before the verdict has anything to do with what was actually built.

That is a strange place to have arrived at. We do not write off all human-written software because one team, somewhere, once let a bad migration take down a production database; we understand that mistakes happen and what matters is whether they were caught, owned, and fixed. AI-assisted development deserves exactly that same standard, and no more suspicion than the standard already provides for. It is not a phase we are waiting out. It is already how a great deal of serious software gets built, ours included, and judging the method instead of the result gets the order of operations backwards.

What actually earned trust, in the end, was never the fact that an AI helped write the code. It was what we did the afternoon something went wrong: how fast we caught it, how completely we fixed it, and what we changed afterward so it could not happen the same way twice. That is the whole test, the only one that has ever mattered for any piece of software, and it has nothing to do with who or what held the pen. Somewhere out there tonight, someone is still doing quiet arithmetic on a stranger's timeline, trying to guess at their own. That is who this was always for.
